Good Morning

Thursday, February 19, 2015

Obama talks extremism, a superbug at UCLA and more in today's Briefing.

What the Media Will Keep Talking About Today

President Obama waded into controversial territory at a White House summit on violent extremism on Wednesday. "We are not at war with Islam. We are at war with people who have perverted Islam", he told the crowd.

The Obama administration has been butchered by many in the press for refusing to characterize ISIS as "Islamic." Obama maintains that ISIS members are terrorists who wear the cloak of religion to curry support. But others, including some Democrats, believe Obama is favoring political correctness over political reality by refusing to acknowledge ISIS's religious motivations.

Obama called on Muslim leaders to more stridently criticize groups like ISIS and to avoid falling into the mindset that the U.S. is intent on suffocating Islam.

If Hell Froze Over

It would look like much of the eastern half of the United States.

Just take a look at the low temperatures expected this morning: Chicago (-7), Cleveland (-1), Fargo, North Dakota (-23), Louisville (-4), Nashville (-2), and St. Louis (0). Note: Those aren't wind chills, which are too depressing to address.

Meanwhile, Bostonians are losing their minds. The city was slammed by a record-breaking 90+ inches of snow from Jan. 19 to Feb. 17. Boston natives have resorted to jumping out of their windows, only to disappear into snow mountains. Boston Mayor Marty Walsh told the window flyers to cut it out.

In addition to Winter Storm Octavia, two separate storms are expected to hit parts of the Northeast (today) and the South (over the weekend). Godspeed.

If Hospitals Make You Queasy

Then keep scrolling. The UCLA Medical Center discovered a drug-resistant 'superbug' that may have been exposed to 179 people, infected seven patients, and contributed to two deaths.

The so-called 'superbug' is actually a nasty bacteria known as CRE. Health officials are investigating medical instruments in relation to the transmission of CRE, specifically endoscopes. The Los Angeles Times reported that endoscopes are inserted in one's throat and can pass CRE from one patient to another, even after being disinfected.

Endoscopes are used to treat certain forms of cancer, gallstones and digestive-related diseases on 500,000 patients annually. Similar CRE outbreaks have been reported in the past.

An Extension After All?

The Greek government's recent tough talk about abandoning its bailout agreement with the European Union may have been just that: talk.

Reuters reported that Greece wants a six-month bailout extension with a number of concessions to the EU. Why? Mostly because Greece is running out of cash.
